Reflections from the Great Divide Mountain Bike Trail (2 of 10) – Canada

Sun 26 July 2009
olliebray
Tags Canada refelections olliebray

[][]

This is the second of a series of ten posts about my recent trip to Mountain Bike the Continental Divide between Banff, Canada to Antelope Wells, New Mexico. 

I arrived late in Banff and in hindsight I should have had a days rest and preparation before heading out on the trail. Instead, after I arrived I stayed up late and with the help of the Simon and Marco put my bike and trailer together and sorted out our equipment.

Day 1: Banff - Chester Lake Trailhead (43 miles)

Before starting the route I posted my bike bag and a few things that I didn’t need on to Phoenix, Arizona. As with previous trip I posted my stuff to the Greyhound Bus Terminal in Phoenix. Quite often people don’t know that you can do this but actually as long as you are not in hurry for your package to arrive, Greyhound is a pretty reliable service for parcels and they specialise in taking oversized and overweight packages (like bike bags).

We eventually got started at 10am – the first day did not go well for me. I was exhausted from work, the trans-Atlantic flight and the sudden change of routine. I think my traveling companions were annoyed with me, perhaps thinking that I was un-fit and unprepared rather than just tired from travel. This aside the scenery was varied an beautiful and I new that we were going to be in for a real treat over the next month.

We stopped at about 7pm – I was happy to go on further but I was only going at 6mph and I could sense the frustration of my slowness on Simon and Marco. In any case we stopped, we ate, we hydrated and we slept – I slept really well, catching up on vital hours of rest.

Day 2: Chester Lake Trailhead - Elkford (66 miles)

Waking the next morning I felt a lot better and although I felt stronger as I cycled out of the campsite this didn’t last long as I had a puncture within about 15 minutes. Luckily the slime in my tires sealed the hole and I was able to fix it relatively quickly.

The days riding was mainly on gravel backcountry roads and also included our first Continental Divide Crossing as we ascended Elk Pass. I’m not sure if it was because it was the first divide crossing but in my head Elk Pass still rates as one of the hardest crossings of the continental divide that we completed during our month away.

[caption id=”” align=”aligncenter” width=”375” caption=”Elk Pass”][/caption]

During the decent from Elk Pass I got a blow out on my front tire which sent me over my handlebars and crashing to the ground. Stupidly I had my hands on my t-grips rather than my breaks during the decent but luckily apart from a few minor scratches both my bike and I were relatively unharmed.

My luck continued to plummet as during the next decent both my water bottles came flying out of my bottle cages and I didn’t notice until I had got to the base of the hill. There was no way that I was going up to retrieve them as I didn’t have a clue at which part of the two mile decent they had decided to leave me.

I was also developing some sort of stomach bug and the saddle sores were starting to get pretty big!

That evening we camped at the Elkford Lions Club Camp Ground and had pizza at a small café over the road. We made plans for the next day as the route had changed. We decided that Marco (the mountain goat) would take the new route. The new route (developed in 2009) involved a mountain pass crossing and a section of hike-a-bike where as Simon and I would stick to the original route and maps.

Back at the campsite we had a bit of gear sort to make sure that the two teams could be self-sufficient over the next 48 hours.

Day 3: Elkford – Baynes Lake (78 miles)

There was no doubt about it I was feeling stronger and almost completely recovered from the jet-lag, but I still really suffering from my stomach bug which at times was incredibly painful.

Much of today’s route was on paved roads and gravel track. The best news however was that apart from the 2 mile steep ‘killer’ climb out of Elkford most of the route was downhill!

[][]After a couple of hours we stopped at the town of Sparwood for a bite to eat. Sparwood was a mining town and at the centre of the town was ‘Titan’the worlds biggest truck that would have been used at some point to extract coal from under the ground. Sparwood was also the location where Marco headed off on the new route for 24 hours. He didn’t complete the route due to poor weather and has written an account of his experience here – which reassures me that I made the right decision not to go with him!

Simon and I continued toFernie – where we had another sandwich. On the way Simon got a flat tire in his BOB trailer and changed it by the side of the road. Little did we know that this would only be the start of the problems with the BOB and the pump!

We were cycling well (although it was mostly downhill!) and I was keen to make today from a psychological point of view my first 100 mile day. However, in the end we decided to stop at the campsite at Baynes Lake rather than pushing on the extra 24 miles to the next campsite.

The good thing about the Baynes Lake campsite was that it had access to the Internet and I was able to check on Marco’s location using the SPOT GPS locator that he was carrying. It was great to see that he had made his target for the day – although he had a huge day ahead of him to follow!

During the night it started to rain, and rain, and rain, and……rain!

Day 4: Baynes Lake – Eureka (?? miles)

It was cold when we woke up and still raining heavily. We got moving fairly quickly and packed our stuff under the front of someone’s trailer. We were just about to leave when we noticed that Simons BOB wheel had gone down again. We pumped it up but five minutes later it was down again. We changed the tube and pumped it up but again after a few minutes it went down again.

It took us a while to work it out. But in the end we concluded it must be a problem with the pump – it would only inflate the tire to a certain air pressure and then it would deflate? Sort of options we managed to find someone on the campsite who had an electric pump – we pumped up the BOB and headed off ‘hand pump-less!’.

As we made our way towards the US border it continued to rain and we sank into the mud on the forest tracks. We were also very nervous about getting punctures as we would be un-able to fix them. Eventually we made it to Grasmere where we enjoyed (well ate) some lumberjack sandwiches and Simon threw his peanuts all over the floor of the shop which caused the locals great amusement!

[caption id=”” align=”aligncenter” width=”375” caption=”Heading to the US Border”][/caption]

Continuing south we were now cycling through the Tobacco Plain Indian Reservation. It got its name as the Native American Indians had once grown tobacco on these lands. However, after the colonisation the process was never able to be repeated by the white settlers.

We had a relatively easy border crossing at Roosville despite the rather grumpy boarder guard who was struggling to understand the difference between green and read.

From the border we continued south to the town of Eureka where we had agreed to meet up with Marco. At Eureka we based ourselves at a little café and waited, and waited and waited. I cycled down into the town and managed to pick up the wifi signal from the local library (which was shut) on my iPhone. I could see Marco’s location on the SPOT and I took a few screen shots to show Simon.

I couple of hours later we re-checked his location and he hadn’t really moved. Puzzled, we decided to book ourselves into a motel where would be able to monitor his progress without huddling outside the library in the rain! Eventually he started to move (in the wrong direction) see his blog post for details as it sounded like a crazy day! Eventually he got to us at about 21.30h and we were very pleased to see him and interested to hear about his adventures.
